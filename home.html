Alex Vollo @AlexVolloFPL · 11m

Seeing a lot of Schade vs Dango debates framed purely around positional charts, but let's actually look at what the numbers are telling us👇

When you look at Burnley's defensive profile, the obvious reaction is to target the flanks. But once you split the data properly, the picture becomes more nuanced, and that's where the decision between Schade and Ouattara gets interesting.

Burnley have been one of the most vulnerable sides in the league when it comes to expected threat conceded to left wingers. That means teams are able to progress down that side. Territory is gained. The ball moves into valuable zones. Those are sustained advancement. Structurally, that flank can be built against.

On the other side, the profile shifts. Against right wingers, Burnley don't concede especially high expected threat. The progression numbers are fairly average. But xG conceded to right wingers is among the highest in the league. That split matters, let me explain you why.

High xT conceded suggests repeated entries into dangerous areas, sustained pressure, volume, involvement. High xG conceded without high xT suggests something fewer situations, but more decisive ones: isolation, transitions, all that stuff. Moments that skip buildup and go straight to shots.

So it’s always not just about "which side is weaker?" It’s about what kind of weakness exists. Especially if we're talking about predicting events.

Schade is at his best when he can attack space repeatedly. When his side can pin the opposition back and progress down the flank. When he gets multiple touches in advanced zones and can build rhythm into his game. Burnley's right-side profile hints at exactly that kind of scenario.

Ouattara fits a different script. He doesn't necessarily need territory. If Burnley's left side collapses in moments, particularly when isolated or caught in transition, that aligns with a winger who thrives on direct, shot-heavy actions rather than volume.

So it becomes a question of match state. If you expect Brentford to dominate possession and control territory, Schade likely sees more involvement. If the game opens up, becomes transitional, or features isolated duels, Ouattara's side may produce the higher-value chances.

Historically, Burnley's outcomes in those two game states look very different. That's why I always say that when we look at numbers, we always need to contextualise them. Data only becomes useful when you understand the type of situation it's describing. Otherwise, it's just garbage numbers.
